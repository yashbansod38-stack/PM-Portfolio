With the rise of large language models, thousands of startups have bolted "AI" onto their platforms. Yet, a vast majority of these features see less than 5% adoption rates. Here’s why most AI products fail and how to build ones that succeed.

## The Chatbot Phenomenon

When ChatGPT broke records, the default UX for "adding AI" became a chat widget. But chat is a high-cognitive-load interface. Users have to:
1. Know exactly what they want.
2. Figure out the right magic words (prompts) to get it.
3. Review the AI's output to make sure it isn't hallucinating.

If your product reduces a 3-click task into a 5-minute conversation, you haven't improved the UX. You've degraded it.

## Invisible AI is the Best AI

The best AI products don't look like AI at all. They look like better workflows.

Take Notion's auto-tags or auto-fill properties. The user doesn't write a prompt; the system just knows the context and fills in the blanks. The magic happens behind a simple UI element, not a glowing textbox.

## Problem First, Not Prompt First

When building AI capabilities, the rules of product management don't change. 
- What is the user's core job-to-be-done?
- How much faster or cheaper can we make it using AI?
- Where is the human-in-the-loop required for safety or trust?

If the feature doesn't solve a real problem significantly better than the standard non-AI way, it's a gimmick.
